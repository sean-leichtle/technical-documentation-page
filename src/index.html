The Basic Method

A proof by natural deduction (PND) requires a listing and numbering of premises as well as a statement of the conclusion to be derived. Usually, this conclusion is preceded by the symbols "/∴".

Then, the proof itself follows by means of a series of numbered steps each of which is derived by referring to a previous step or previous steps as well as to a rule of substitution or a rule of inference. This process is reiterated until the conclusion is derived in a like manner.

Rules of Replacement or Substitution

These rules are logically stronger than the rules of inference formulated below. Since all rules of substitution entail equivalencies, the "whole-step" condition of rules of inference does not apply. This means, for instance, that if "p > q" appears as either a step or part of a step, "~q > ~p" can be substituted for it according to transposition.

  • Commutativity (Comm.): (p & q) :: (q & p); (p v q) :: (q v p)
  • Association (Assoc.): (p & (q & r)) :: ((p & q) & r); (p v (q v r)) :: ((p v q) v r)
  • Tautology (Taut.): (p & p) :: p; (p v p) :: p
  • Distribution (Dist.): (p & (q v r)) :: ((p & q) v (p & r)); (p v (q & r)) :: ((p v q) & (p v r))
  • Double Negation (DN): p :: ~~p
  • Transposition (Trans.): (p > q) :: (~q > ~p)
  • Implication (Imp.): (p > q) :: (~p v q)
  • DeMorgan's Rule (DM): ~(p & q) :: (~p v ~q); ~(p v q) :: (~p & ~q)
  • Equivalence (Equiv.): (p <=> q) :: ((p > q) & (q > p))
  • Exportation (Exp.): ((p & q) > r) :: (p > (q > r))

Rules of Inference

Rules of inference may only be applied to entire steps of a proof. Thus if using Modus Ponens, nothing but "p > q" and "p" must exhaustively comprise two entire, separate steps. If either "p > q" or "p" or both form only parts of entire steps of a proof, they cannot be used in conjunction with Modus Ponens.

  • Modus Ponens (MP): From "p > q" and "p", infer "q"
  • Modus Tollens (MT): From "p > q" and "~q", infer "~p"
  • Disjunctive Syllogism (DS): From "p v q" and "~p", infer "q"
  • Hypothetical Syllogism (HS): From "p > q" and "q > r", infer "p > r"
  • Conjunction (Conj.): From "p" and "q", infer "p & q"
  • Simplification (Simp.): From "p & q", infer p
  • Constructive Dilemma (CD): From "p > q", "r > s" and "p v r", infer "q v s"
  • Introduction (Intro.)**: From "p", infer "p v q"

**The Introduction rule seems, on the face of it, illicit. Nevertheless it represents a valid inference if one considers that a disjunction as a whole is true just in case one of its disjuncts is true. Thus, if "p" is true, then "p v q" will also be true regardless of the truth of "q".

Strategies of Formal Proof

Direct Proof (DP):

  1. P1
  2. P2 / ∴ C
  3. deduced WFF + line number + deductive rule
  4. ...
  5. QED: line number + deductive rule

Indirect Proof (IP):

  1. P1
  2. P2 / ∴ C
  3. ~C
  4. deduced WFF + line number + deductive rule
  5. ...
  6. F & ~F + line number + deductive rule
  7. + 1. QED: lines 3 to n + deductive rule

Conditional Proof (CP):

  1. P1
  2. P2 / ∴ F -> G
  3. F (provisionally assumed) / ∴ G
  4. ...
  5. deduced WFF + line number + deductive rule
  6. G + line number + deductive rule
  7. + 1. F -> G (lines 3 to n, Conditional Proof)

Examples

Direct Proof:

  1. (B v C) -> ~A
  2. B / ∴ ~A
  3. B v C (from 2, Intro.)
  4. ~A (from 1 and 3, MP)

Indirect Proof:

  1. (A v B) -> ~A /∴ ~A
  2. ~~A (assumption indirect proof)
  3. ~(A v B) (from 1 and 2, MT)
  4. ~A & ~B (from 3, DM)
  5. ~A (from 4, Simp.)
  6. A (from 2, DN)
  7. A & ~A (from 5 and 6, Conj.)
  8. ~A (lines 2 to 7, IP)

Conditional Proof:

  1. A -> (B v C)
  2. ~C / ∴ (A -> B)
  3. A (assumption indirect proof)
  4. B v C (from 1 and 3, MP)
  5. B (from 2 and 4, DS)
  6. A -> B (from 3 to 5, CP)
